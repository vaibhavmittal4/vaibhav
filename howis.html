How is Childhood

Cancer Treated?

Childhood cancer is typically treated using a combination of different approaches, including surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, and stem cell transplantation.

Surgery

Surgery is often the initial step in treating childhood cancer. It involves removing the tumor and surrounding tissues to the extent possible. The surgeon may also remove nearby lymph nodes for examination.

Immunotherapy

Immunotherapy helps harness the body's immune system to recognize and destroy cancer cells. It can involve the use of immune checkpoint inhibitors, monoclonal antibodies, or CAR-T cell therapy, among other approaches.

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy involves the use of powerful drugs to kill cancer cells or stop their growth. These drugs can be given orally, through injections, or via intravenous infusion.

Targeted Therapy

Targeted therapies are drugs that specifically target certain molecules or genetic abnormalities present in cancer cells. They interfere with specific pathways or proteins that are crucial for cancer growth and survival.

Stem Cell Transplantation

Stem cell transplantation is a procedure in which healthy stem cells are infused into the body to replace damaged or destroyed bone marrow.

Radiation Therapy

Radiation therapy uses high-energy beams, such as X-rays or protons, to target and destroy cancer cells. It is often used after surgery to eliminate any remaining cancer cells or in cases where surgery is not possible.
